Martin Luther King Jr. was assassinated in Memphis, but his death reverberated across the United States.

Memphis did not feel the full flames of riots in April 1968, in part because of King's organizing appa­ratus. But his message of nonviolence was not enough to hold back the tide of rage that brought millions of dollars of damage to other major metropolises, including Chicago and Baltimore.

Using techniques and film formats associated with late-20th-century documentary photography, and shooting from a helicopter, LaToya Ruby Frazier revisited Memphis, Chicago, and Baltimore to explore how they have responded to five more decades of oppression.

Memphis

The Lorraine Hotel, where King was assassinated, has since been reinvisioned as the National Civil Rights Museum, which preserves many of the exact details from the day of the murder. A Dodge and a Cadillac are parked in the lot below room 306; a white wreath on the railing honors the room's most famous occupant.

Memphis

The assassin James Earl Ray shot King, who was standing on the balcony from a flophouse bathroom across the street.

Memphis

The Memphis Pyramid, one of Tennessee's most famous buildings, overlooks the Hernando DeSoto Bridge across the Mississippi River. Black Lives Matter protesters shut down the bridge in 2016, echoing sanitation workers' demands in 1968 for economic justice and calling for an end to police brutality.

Chicago

The structural inequality of Chicago is striking even today. Just west of the Magnificent Mile marks the location where the Cabrini-Green public-housing projects once stood.

It is currently being redeveloped. But in 1968, rage gripped the ghetto, and snipers reportedly fired on police from the towers.

Chicago

In 1968, King led sustained demonstrations against housing discrimination around the city. During a march into the Gage Park neighborhood, protestors were met by white counterprotesters, who pelted King with rocks.

Chicago

The Martin Luther King Jr. Living Memorial, commemorating the incident, was unveiled in Marquette Park in 2016.

Baltimore

The 2015 Baltimore Uprising saw the city's most extensive protests since 1968's Holy Week Uprising.

In 1968, the disturbances started on Gay Street, where a crowd of people had gathered. After the windows of a hat shop were smashed, fires and looting broke out.

Baltimore

The CVS drugstore at the corner of North and Pennsylvania Avenues was burned during the 2015 unrest.

Baltimore

On April 19, 2015, Freddie Gray, 25, died from an injury suffered in police custody, sparking protests throughout the city. Murals in his honor were painted near the Gilmor Homes, the public housing project where he had been arrested.

Baltimore

Disturbances occurred throughout the Sandtown-Winchester neighborhood.

Baltimore

The despair of 1968 left an indelible mark on Baltimore's history. As in Chicago, unrest in Baltimore's black neighborhoods cemented white flight to the suburbs and other parts of the city.

Baltimore

The divestment and discriminatory lending practices—infamously associated with Baltimore—increased the segregation and deterioration of black neighborhoods, like the one where Freddie "Pepper" Gray grew up in poverty.

LaToya Ruby Frazier is an artist whose work in photography, video, and performance builds visual archives that address industrialism, Rust Belt revitalization, environmental justice, health-care equity, family, and communal history. She was awarded a MacArthur Fellowship in 2015.
